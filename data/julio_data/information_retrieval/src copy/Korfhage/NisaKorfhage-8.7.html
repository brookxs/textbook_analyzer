8.7 Satisfaction and Frustration, and Related Measure

One of the problems using recall and precision is that such measures fail to take into account the sequentiality effect in presenting retrieval results. The effects of sequentiality are:

  • The examination of one document affects the user's judgment of all documents that follow it.
  • Mixed documents of relevant and nonrelevant documents may stop users from retrieving relevant documents that follow nonrelevant documents.

Normalized recall is an ideal system to which all relevant documents are retrieved before any of the nonrelevant ones. A step function graph is developed such that an ideal graph is a graph that will rise one unit as each of the relevant documents is retrieved, to a maximum value equal to the number of relevant documents in the collection. The difference between an actual graph and an ideal graph is a measure of the performance of the retrieval system. A normalized precision can also be developed on the same idea as the normalized recall.

The sliding ratio is another measure similar to normalized recall but differ in two ways:

  1. It is developed on the basis of weighted relevance judgments.
  2. It is based on the retrieval of N documents for some specified value of N, rather than on the full set of relevant documents.

There are three measures similar to sliding ratio:

  1. Satisfaction measure, which considers only the relevant documents.
  2. Frustration measure, which considers only the nonrelevant documents.
  3. Total measure, a weighted combination of satisfaction and frustration.
