5.8 Stop Lists

Common terms have two impacts on information retrieval systems:

  • In any measurements depending on word frequencies, the very high frequencies of these stop words tend to diminish the impact of frequency differences among less common words.
  • These words carry little meaning by themselves that produce large amount of unproductive processing. For these reasons, it is common to define a stop list, or negative dictionary, consisting of such words.

When a text is initially processed, each word occurrence is checked against the stop list. If the word is found there, it is ignored in any further processing. In organizing a retrieval system that uses phrases, the use of stop words must be carefully considered, as these are frequently words that identify significant phrases.

To perform the stop list checking, it takes time. Various techniques and algorithms are used to minimize processing time. Hashing technique is a technique focused on being able to determine whether a word is on a stop list with just one computation and one comparison, and hence, reduce time in processing. Stop lists can also be structured into a trie where each word is checked character by character against the stop list.
